About

Bio

Alina is a Hong Kong–based urban designer and researcher with a rare combination of skills usually split across different people. She analyses cities with hard spatial methods — remote-sensing heat-island assessments, space-syntax studies of informal settlements, green-index and morphology mapping. She designs them, from district masterplans in Milan and Hong Kong to place-branding systems. And she researches how they are lived in, with peer-reviewed work on ethnicity and homelessness and an EEG study of the emotional response to high-density housing.

Trained in the Russian/post-Soviet urbanist tradition — KB Strelka, the Genplan Institute of Moscow, STEP, and a top-ranked urban-planning degree at HSE — she now works across Asia, giving her a double lens few people hold. What connects everything is one conviction: cities should be designed from how people actually live in them, not from a single template — and the data, design tools, and AI to do that finally exist. She is completing her Master of Urban Design at the University of Hong Kong, where her current research uses EEG to test how the feeling of 'home' differs across cultures.
