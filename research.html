Flagship — Traces of Life

Master of Urban Design, HKU · in progress

An EEG and behavioural study of affective response to old versus new public housing in Hong Kong.

Master's research in progress, The University of Hong Kong (Urban Design), supervised by Dr Alice Vialard with Dr Waishan Qiu and Dr Bin Jiang. It started from a simple observation: an old housing estate, soon to be demolished, felt more alive than the polished new towers built to replace it — because of the visible traces of inhabitation residents leave behind. The study introduces Inhabited Visual Complexity (IVC): the visual richness that accumulates through decades of living — laundry, plants, grilles, signage, small personal things — and asks whether it is part of what makes older estates feel welcoming rather than threatening. The planned design pairs EEG (frontal alpha asymmetry, frontal alpha and beta; Emotiv EPOC X) with self-report (SAM, PRS-11) and an online perception survey pre-registered on OSF and run with Prolific, comparing two old estates and two new developments across six shared-space typologies. The main comparison uses real, controlled photographs normalised in CIELAB for luminance and colour so that era, rather than image exposure or palette, drives the comparison, with an IVC gradient generated by inpainting kept as a separate, clearly labelled condition. Semantic segmentation yields roughly forty morphological measures — inhabited-element density, visual entropy, green view index, enclosure — that feed linear mixed-effects models with estate as a random effect. So far only a small behavioural pilot has data; the behavioural strand is under way and the EEG strand is still to come.

Early signal · EEG to come

A behavioural pilot of about sixty people suggests the older estates may feel best at a middle level of inhabitation — not empty, not crowded — and that a sense of resident agency is moderately associated with how pleasant a place feels (r ≈ 0.5). The accumulated traces of life may be part of what makes an older estate feel more restorative; the EEG measures, not yet collected, will test whether physiological response follows.
